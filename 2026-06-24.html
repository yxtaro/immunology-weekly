The title is A guardian role of TagA in protecting Mycobacterium tuberculosis from nitrosative killing. The first author is Ying Zhang from the Shanghai Public Health Clinical Center and Shanghai Institute of Infectious Diseases and Biosecurity at Fudan University in Shanghai. So here is what this study is all about. When your immune system gets activated, macrophages, which are the big front-line immune cells that gobble up pathogens, generate large amounts of reactive nitrogen species. Think of these as chemical weapons your body uses to damage the DNA of bacteria hiding inside those cells, specifically Mycobacterium tuberculosis, or Mtb for short, the bacterium that causes tuberculosis. The idea is that this DNA damage stops the bacteria from replicating. But here is the thing: scientists have not fully understood how Mtb manages to repair that damage and survive anyway. In this study, the researchers used a technique called transposon insertion sequencing to figure out which genes Mtb needs to survive inside different types of macrophages. And they found a really interesting one called tagA. This gene showed a specialized role in helping Mtb survive specifically in M1-polarized macrophages, which are the most inflammatory and aggressive type of macrophage. They also found this role mattered in mice about four weeks after infection, right when macrophages tend to shift toward that M1 state. Mechanistically, what does TagA actually do? It turns out TagA helps Mtb resist damage from a DNA-alkylating agent by excising a specific damaged base called 3-methyladenine, or 3-MA. A key amino acid residue called Glu48 is critical for this enzymatic activity. And crucially, TagA was found to protect the Mtb genome from alkylation damage caused by nitrosative stress, which is precisely the kind of hostile chemical environment that M1 macrophages create. When the researchers blocked an enzyme called inducible nitric oxide synthase, or iNOS, either with a drug in mice or by genetically deleting the nos2a gene in zebrafish, the survival defect seen in Mtb strains lacking TagA was largely rescued. So in other words, without the nitric oxide stress, it did not matter as much whether TagA was there. Together, these findings reveal a previously unappreciated mechanism by which this DNA repair enzyme protects tuberculosis bacteria against 3-MA DNA damage under nitrosative stress, ultimately helping the bacteria survive inside those aggressive inflammatory macrophages and during real infections in living animals. The first author is Praveen Barrodia from the Department of Experimental Radiation Oncology at the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center in Houston. Now this one is a beautiful story that connects fasting, gut bacteria, and the regeneration of your intestinal lining. The researchers identified a bacterial species called Akkermansia muciniphila, often abbreviated as AKK, as a key mediator of this protective response. When mice were fasted, AKK became enriched in their guts. And here is the kicker: when the researchers used antibiotics to deplete AKK, the protective effect of fasting disappeared. When they reintroduced AKK, both organismal survival and intestinal integrity were restored. That is pretty compelling evidence that this one bacterial species is doing a lot of heavy lifting. Medium from AKK cultures and propionate itself induced histone H3 acetylation in intestinal stem cell cultures. The fourth paper is also from PNAS, June 30th, 2026, titled Immune cell-intrinsic STING activation drives tumor ferroptosis via AA-mediated suppression of ACSL4 lactylation in colorectal cancer. The first author is Lina Ding from Xuzhou Medical University in China. Ferroptosis is a form of iron-dependent cell death that has emerged as an important mechanism in antitumor immunity.

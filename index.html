Weekly Summary

Infection and Antimicrobial Immunity

Mycobacterium tuberculosis has evolved clever ways to survive inside immune cells, and a new study sheds light on one of them. Researchers identified a gene called tagA that helps the bacterium repair DNA damage caused by reactive nitrogen species released by activated macrophages. Without this repair mechanism, tuberculosis bacteria are far more vulnerable to being killed, pointing to tagA as a potential target for new therapies.

A specialized subset of neutrophils marked by the protein Mrgpra2 plays a previously unappreciated role in fighting bone marrow infections. These cells deploy neutrophil extracellular traps, sticky webs of DNA and proteins that ensnare bacteria, but only when two signals are present simultaneously: defensin proteins and TNF-alpha. This dual-signal requirement ensures the response is precisely timed and context-appropriate, helping control infections like osteomyelitis without triggering unnecessary damage.

When Salmonella invades the gut, the host fights back partly by starving the bacterium of the metals it needs to survive. A protein called SLC11A2 in intestinal epithelial cells actively sequesters iron and other divalent metals away from the pathogen, limiting its ability to grow and cause disease. This work deepens our understanding of nutritional immunity, the strategy by which the body controls metal availability as a frontline defense against infection.

A new study on dengue antibodies tracked the immune responses of infected individuals over eighteen months, profiling 84 distinct antibody subsets. Different antibody populations showed remarkably varied kinetics depending on what part of the dengue virus they targeted and what antibody class they belonged to. These findings are especially relevant for vaccine design given that cross-reactive antibodies from a previous dengue infection can sometimes worsen disease upon reinfection with a different viral strain.

Children begin life carrying maternal antibodies but quickly build their own antiviral immune memory. Using high-resolution profiling of antibody responses, researchers found that infants lose maternal antibodies rapidly and replace them through roughly 22 new viral encounters in their first three years. Antibody responses remain highly dynamic until around age seven, after which the repertoire stabilizes into patterns more similar to adults.

New structural work on influenza antibodies reveals that protection does not always require a virus to be neutralized directly. Three antibodies encoded by the same gene segment all targeted the conserved stem region of the hemagglutinin protein, yet two of them offered strong protection in animals primarily through Fc-mediated effector functions rather than direct neutralization. This finding highlights the importance of considering how antibodies recruit immune cells and other effectors when evaluating vaccine candidates.

Cancer Immunology

A new study uncovers an unexpected connection between immune cell activation and a form of cancer cell death called ferroptosis in colorectal cancer. When the innate immune sensor STING is activated inside immune cells, it triggers the release of arachidonic acid, which then interferes with a chemical modification on the tumor cell protein ACSL4, ultimately making cancer cells more susceptible to ferroptosis. This cross-talk between immune metabolism and tumor cell death opens potential new strategies for enhancing cancer treatment.

Copper-dependent cell death, known as cuproptosis, interacts with the immune system in ways that could be harnessed for cancer therapy. CD8 T cells were found to make tumor cells more sensitive to cuproptosis, and in turn cuproptotic tumor cells release signals that further activate immune responses, creating a reinforcing loop. Importantly, tumors that resist immunotherapy were found to suppress copper metabolism pathways, and overcoming this suppression restored both cuproptosis and immune activity.

CAR T cell therapy is transformative but frequently fails because most infused cells do not survive long enough to provide lasting remission. A new study shows that the costimulatory domains built into CAR T cell constructs, either 4-1BB or CD28, directly influence whether early cell divisions produce long-lived memory-like daughters or short-lived effector cells through a process of asymmetric division. Understanding how these signals shape fate decisions early on could guide the design of more durable CAR T cell therapies.

The transcription factor IRF8 has been identified as a key driver of T cell exhaustion specifically in the tumor setting. IRF8 is highly expressed in tumor-reactive CD8 T cells but not during chronic viral infection, and its activity is suppressed by sustained type I interferon signaling, which is common in infection but less prominent in tumors. This distinction may help explain why T cell exhaustion in cancer and in chronic infection, despite appearing similar, have different underlying molecular programs.

STING agonists hold promise as cancer immunotherapies, but they also damage the activated T cells needed to fight tumors. Researchers discovered that activated T cells upregulate a transporter protein called SLC7A1 that inadvertently allows cGAMP and related STING agonists to enter the cell, triggering toxicity. Blocking this transporter could help preserve T cells during STING-based treatments, improving their effectiveness in combination with checkpoint blockade therapies.

A comprehensive molecular profiling study of PEComas, a rare type of tumor, has revealed distinct biological subtypes based on their genetic and cellular features. Gynecologic PEComas frequently carry mutations in the cyclin-dependent kinase module, while tumors at other sites often lack mTOR pathway alterations and instead show stem cell-like gene expression programs. These findings lay the groundwork for a molecular classification that could guide more targeted treatment decisions for patients with these rare tumors.

Autoimmunity

New research shows that B cells in lupus-prone individuals have an unusually active pyrimidine synthesis pathway, and that this metabolic rewiring is important for driving the autoimmune response. Blocking pyrimidine synthesis selectively dampened the abnormal germinal center and plasma cell responses seen in lupus without impairing normal responses to foreign antigens. This metabolic vulnerability in autoreactive B cells could be exploited to develop more targeted therapies for systemic lupus erythematosus.

A study of liver disease finds that when macrophages in the bile ducts engulf dying cells loaded with toxic bile acids, they accumulate those bile acids internally, which skews their inflammatory behavior. Rather than returning to a tissue-restoring state after clearing dead cells, these macrophages become altered in ways that may perpetuate inflammation in conditions like primary sclerosing cholangitis. The findings suggest that the cargo inside dying cells can fundamentally change how the immune system responds to cell death.

Regulatory T cells, normally suppressors of immune responses, can transform into helper-like cells that actually support protective immunity during repeated malaria infections. During a first malaria infection these cells dampen germinal center reactions and impair parasite control, but memory regulatory T cells left behind after that infection switch roles upon re-exposure, acquiring functions that support rather than suppress protective responses. This plasticity has important implications for malaria vaccine strategies.

Transplant Immunology and Immune Tolerance

A modified version of interleukin-2, engineered to preferentially expand regulatory T cells while sparing other immune cells, successfully promoted transplant acceptance in mice. The molecule selectively expanded a population of regulatory T cells marked by the protein ST2, and this expansion was associated with antigen-specific tolerance rather than broad immunosuppression. These findings support the further development of engineered IL-2 molecules as a safer alternative to conventional immunosuppressive drugs in transplantation.

Gut and Mucosal Immunity

The gut microbiome plays a much broader role in antimicrobial resistance than previously appreciated, acting as a reservoir of resistance genes and a competitive arena that shapes which resistant bacteria can persist and spread. A new modeling framework integrates ecological and evolutionary perspectives to capture how microbial communities influence the emergence and transmission of resistance at scales ranging from individual patients to entire populations. The authors argue that understanding these community-level dynamics is essential for controlling the global spread of antibiotic resistance.

Research on bacterial vaginosis has produced a detailed genomic catalogue of Gardnerella, the key bacterial genus associated with this common and clinically significant condition. Sequencing nearly 400 isolates revealed new plasmids and bacteriophages, clarified species boundaries, and showed that different Gardnerella species have distinct metabolic and virulence profiles. These insights should improve diagnostic precision and help identify which Gardnerella strains are most likely to cause harm.

Innate Immunity and Inflammation

A review article in the Journal of Experimental Medicine draws attention to the underappreciated role of physical forces in shaping immune responses. Tissue stiffness, shear stress, and mechanical stretch all influence how innate immune cells behave by engaging conserved molecular pathways that translate physical signals into inflammatory programs. The authors argue that when these mechanical cues go awry, for instance due to injury or disease, the resulting dysregulation can drive chronic inflammation, making mechanobiology a promising new frontier in immunology.

When RNA viruses infect cells, small fat droplets form inside the cell and dock against the mitochondria, and this physical contact turns out to be critical for mounting an antiviral response. The fat droplet protein PLIN3 binds to a mitochondrial fusion protein called MFN2, releasing a brake on the antiviral signaling protein MAVS and allowing it to form the aggregates needed to trigger interferon production. This unexpected link between cellular fat metabolism and innate antiviral signaling adds a new layer of regulation to our understanding of how cells detect and respond to viral infection.

A bacterially derived toxin called lipopolysaccharide can cause life-threatening inflammation even in tiny amounts, and neutralizing it in complex biological fluids has proven difficult. Researchers engineered a nanoparticle system inspired by a plant protein that specifically recognizes and degrades LPS with high efficiency. This nano-chimera approach outperformed existing methods in preclinical models and could offer a new strategy for treating sepsis.

Alveolar macrophages, the immune guardians of the lung, are maintained partly through the recruitment and reprogramming of blood monocytes, but the signals controlling this process have not been fully defined. A new study identifies endothelial cells lining lung blood vessels as early instructors of macrophage fate, using a signaling molecule called DLL4 to set monocytes on the path toward becoming lung-resident macrophages. A cooperative network of four transcription factors integrates this and other local tissue signals to lock in the alveolar macrophage identity.

A ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme called UBE2F has been found to act as a molecular brake on the long-term survival of memory CD8 T cells. Removing this enzyme allowed T cells to better maintain their memory state over time, improving the durability of immune protection against recurring infections and potentially cancer. The finding identifies UBE2F as a new target for strategies aimed at strengthening long-lived immune memory.

Metabolic and Endocrine Immunology

Pancreatic alpha-cells, best known for secreting glucagon to raise blood sugar, also play a surprising role in coordinating insulin release in response to immune and nerve signals. The study found that alpha-cells are required for the insulin secretion that occurs in anticipation of eating, known as the cephalic phase response, as well as for the insulin-stimulating effects of the immune signal IL-1 beta. This positions alpha-cells as a hub integrating metabolic, neural, and immune inputs to regulate blood glucose balance.

A newly described protein target in liver-scarring cells called AREL1 may offer a route to treating the liver fibrosis that accompanies metabolic-associated steatohepatitis. Single-cell sequencing of human liver tissue identified a distinct subpopulation of hepatic stellate cells in fibrotic tissue where AREL1 is active, and targeting it therapeutically reduced fibrosis in preclinical models. Given the rising burden of liver disease driven by metabolic dysfunction, this finding is of considerable clinical interest.

Biological aging, measured using a blood-based index called PhenoAge, appears to be accelerating across successive birth cohorts and is linked to a higher risk of developing cancer at an early age. Among over 150,000 young adults in the UK Biobank, those born in later generations showed measurably faster biological aging, and this accelerated aging was associated with cancers of the gastrointestinal tract, thyroid, uterus, and breast. The results suggest that generational shifts in lifestyle and environmental exposures are leaving biological marks that translate into earlier cancer onset.

HIV and Adolescent Immunity

Adolescents who acquired HIV at birth and are receiving antiretroviral therapy show signs of elevated cardiovascular risk compared with their HIV-negative peers. This heightened risk is accompanied by dysfunction in natural killer cells, a type of innate immune cell important both in antiviral defense and in vascular health. The findings raise concern that even well-controlled perinatal HIV infection may have lasting immune and cardiovascular consequences that require monitoring and management into adolescence and adulthood.

Malaria Vaccines

A study using a humanized mouse model of malaria infection found that combining antibodies targeting two different stages of the parasite's life cycle produces better protection than either alone. Existing licensed vaccines focus solely on the circumsporozoite protein found on sporozoites, the form of the parasite introduced by mosquito bites, but adding antibodies against the blood-stage antigen RH5 significantly boosted protection. This provides concrete experimental support for developing multi-stage malaria vaccines.

Neurodegeneration

A detailed study of amyloid-beta aggregation in Alzheimer's disease reveals that different forms of the protein arising from the same aggregation reaction cause harm through distinct mechanisms. Early aggregates that form before the main fibrils appear were found to disrupt calcium signaling in neurons and impair memory-related synaptic plasticity, while later-stage fibrils triggered a different set of toxic effects. Recognizing that multiple harmful species are present simultaneously may explain why Alzheimer's disease is so difficult to treat with single-target approaches.

A guardian role of TagA in protecting Mycobacterium tuberculosis from nitrosative killing.
Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America | 2026 Jun 30 | Ying Zhang — Shanghai Public Health Clinical Center and Shanghai Institute of Infectious Diseases and Biosecurity, Fudan University, Shanghai 201508, China.

Upon activation, macrophages generate substantial levels of reactive nitrogen species, which can induce alkylating damage in the DNA of intracellular Mycobacterium tuberculosis (Mtb) and thereby restrict bacterial replication. However, the molecular mechanisms by which Mtb repairs such DNA lesions remain poorly understood. Here, we identified genes required for Mtb survival in distinct macrophage subsets using transposon insertion sequencing. Among these, tagA displayed a specialized role in Mtb survival in M1-polarized macrophages, as well as in mice at 4 wk postinfection, a stage when macrophages are biased toward an M1-polarized state. Mechanistically, TagA conferred resistance to the DNA alkylating agent methyl methanesulfonate through its 3-methyladenine (3-MA) excision activity with Glu48 serving as a key catalytic residue for substrate binding. Critically, TagA was found to protect the Mtb genome from alkylation damage caused by nitrosative stress-a hallmark of the M1-polarized macrophage microenvironment. Furthermore, pharmacological inhibition of inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) with S-methylisothiourea sulfate in mice or genetic deletion of nos2a in zebrafish markedly rescued the survival defect of ΔtagA. Together, these findings reveal a previously unappreciated mechanism by which the DNA repair enzyme TagA protects Mtb against 3-MA DNA damage under nitrosative stress, thereby promoting bacterial survival in M1-polarized macrophages and during in vivo infection.
